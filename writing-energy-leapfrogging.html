Barriers to Indonesia’s Energy Leapfrogging in the Southeast Asian Context

21 April 2025 Energy Policy · Governance

I want to share some insights from my research into energy transition, policy, politics, and investment flows — specifically looking at how governance structures and energy market incentives shape the landscape for Indonesia’s energy leapfrogging potential.

This paper explores the concept of “energy leapfrogging” — the possibility for developing countries like Indonesia to bypass the conventional fossil-fuel-dependent energy infrastructure and jump directly to modern renewable energy systems, including microgrids and decentralised generation.

Technology leapfrogging is a concept that refers to the ability of developing countries to skip stages of technological evolution by adopting more advanced technologies directly, bypassing older, less efficient ones.

Key Themes

01

Governance barriers. The energy transition in Indonesia is deeply shaped by governance structures that often favour incumbent fossil-fuel interests, creating systemic barriers to renewable energy adoption.

02

Market incentives. Energy market incentives in Southeast Asia often do not adequately support the economics of renewable energy deployment, particularly for distributed generation and microgrids.

03

Leapfrogging potential. Despite these barriers, Indonesia has significant opportunities for accelerated energy transitions — particularly in rural and island communities where grid extension is costly and unreliable.

04

Policy pathways. Effective policy reform, coupled with targeted investment flows and international cooperation, could unlock Indonesia’s leapfrogging potential within the broader Southeast Asian energy landscape.

Understanding these dynamics is crucial for policymakers, investors, and energy practitioners working to accelerate the clean energy transition in one of the world’s most populous and energy-demanding regions.
