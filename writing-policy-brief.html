Energy from Waste in Scotland: Balancing Capacity with Sustainability

23 February 2025 Policy Brief · Circular Economy

The Scottish Government has a plan to ban biodegradable municipal waste from going into landfills. Many of us might think this isn't a significant issue — we could just incinerate the waste and generate electricity from it. However, this approach could lead to another set of problems.

Shifting focus from recycling to recovery risks undermining recycling efforts and bypassing the waste hierarchy entirely. The possible ban implementation on biodegradable waste will put pressure on unmanaged waste due to the rising amount of waste that can't be sent to landfill. So, the government is currently considering how to balance Energy from Waste (EfW) development with a sustainable framework.

There is a dilemma between ensuring there is enough incineration capacity to comply with the ban and avoiding the construction of too many incinerators — which could result in an overreliance on incineration.

Key Findings

01

Rising incineration pressure. The upcoming ban on biodegradable municipal waste, along with rising landfill tax, has contributed to an increase in incineration as waste is redirected from landfills to EfW facilities.

02

Risk of overcapacity. In 2022, Scotland had six operational EfW sites with 1.1 million tonnes capacity — plus plans for 13 new incinerators adding 2.5 million tonnes, when only 0.7 million tonnes of BMW went to landfill in 2020.

03

Recycling at risk. Surplus capacity could lead to incinerating materials that could be recycled, undermining Scotland's efforts to meet recycling targets.

04

Policy review pending. The Scottish Government review will examine emission reduction, heat reuse, health impacts, and how incineration fits with Scotland's waste hierarchy.

This policy brief highlights the challenge of crafting policies to support the expansion of EfW initiatives while ensuring that we do not build too much capacity, which could disrupt sustainable waste management practices.
